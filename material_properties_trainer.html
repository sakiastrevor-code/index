Ashby · design-limiting properties

Stiff, strong, tough — and six more words
that aren't the same thing.

Eight properties decide whether a material is right for a job. The trick isn't memorising them — it's telling them apart, knowing why a material family ranks where it does, and spotting which one a problem is really about.

The one confusion to kill first

Push down on a long bar resting on two supports. Three different things can go wrong — and people call them all "it's not good enough." They are not the same property.

resists bending — and springs back

Stiffness E

How much it flexes elastically under load. A low-E bar sags a lot but recovers fully when you let go. Nothing is damaged — it's just bendy. Floppy / springy = low E.

how far before damage is permanent

Strength σy

The stress where it stops springing back — it stays bent (yields) or breaks. Push past σy and the damage is permanent. "Bends and stays bent" = passed σy.

does a crack run, or does it stop

Toughness KIc

Resistance to a crack spreading from a flaw. Low toughness = it shatters with no warning, even if it was stiff and strong. Brittle = low KIc.

Why "strong" ≠ "tough" — the example that fixes it

A material can be high on stiffness and strength yet shatter the instant a crack starts. That third axis — toughness — is what "brittle" is really pointing at.

The second confusion: heat flow vs heat speed

steady-state heat throughput

Conductivity λ

Once things settle, how many watts flow through per degree of difference. High λ = a great heat path (copper heat-sink). Low λ = an insulator (foam cup).

how fast the temperature itself moves

Diffusivity a

During the transient, how quickly a temperature change reaches the far side. It folds in heat storage: a = λ / (ρ·Cp). A material can conduct decently yet respond slowly because it stores a lot of heat.

λ answers "how much heat per second?" — a answers "how fast does the warmth travel?" Same idea split into a steady part and a how-quick part.

Mechanical — how it carries load

Stiff (Slack) · Strong (Weak) · Tough (Bros) · Heavy (Light). The aeroplane wing on your slide fails a different way for each one.

Thermal — how it copes with heat

The four pans on your slide: surviving the flame (Tmax), warping (α), heating evenly (λ), and how fast it all happens (a).

Why the rankings come out that way

Almost everything traces back to one thing: how strong the bonds between atoms are. Strong, deep bonds are hard to stretch (stiff), hard to break apart (high melting point), and hold atoms tightly so they barely expand. Read each family as a consequence of its bonding.

Ceramics & glass

strong covalent / ionic bonds, no moving dislocations
  • Ehigh — bonds resist stretching → very stiff
  • σyhigh in compression (hard)
  • KIclow — no plastic flow → brittle, shatters
  • Tmaxhigh — lots of energy to break bonds
  • αlow — atoms sit tight in a deep well

Metals

metallic bonds + free electrons + mobile dislocations
  • Emoderate–high
  • KIchigh — dislocations slip and absorb energy → ductile, tough
  • λ, ahigh — free electrons carry heat fast
  • ρhigh — densely packed heavy atoms
  • αmoderate

Polymers & elastomers

strong chains, but weak van der Waals between them
  • Elow — chains uncoil / slide easily → floppy
  • Tmaxlow — weak secondary bonds soften early
  • αhigh — loose chains, lots of free volume
  • λ, alow — no free electrons → insulators
  • ρlow — light atoms, open structure

Notice the pairings that fall out for free: weak inter-chain bonds explain both low Tmax and high α in polymers. Strong bonds in ceramics explain high E, high Tmax and low α together. Bonding is the hidden variable behind several properties at once.

The vocabulary people actually use, decoded. This is the translation layer between "ceramics are hard" and σy.

They say…They mean…Property

Watch the traps: hard ≠ tough (diamond is both hard and brittle), and strong ≠ stiff (a guitar string is strong but you can deflect it easily).

The two ratios engineers actually plot

Raw properties rarely decide a design — ratios do, because most jobs are "the most X per kilogram." Specific stiffness E/ρ and specific strength σy/ρ are why carbon fibre and aluminium beat steel in aircraft despite steel's higher absolute numbers. Whenever weight matters, divide by ρ before you compare.
